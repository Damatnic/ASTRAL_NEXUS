How to Tie (Around Post):

  1. Wrap rope around post
  2. Cross over standing line
  3. Wrap around post again
  4. Thread working end under last wrap
  5. Tighten

Pros and Cons:

  • ‚úÖ Quick to tie and untie
  • ‚úÖ Adjustable
  • ‚ùå Can slip under load if not backed up
  • ‚ùå Must maintain tension
TAUT-LINE HITCH (Adjustable Loop)

Use: Tent guy lines, adjustable tie-downs, creating adjustable tension

How to Tie:

  1. Wrap working end around object/stake
  2. Wrap working end around standing line 2-3 times (moving toward object)
  3. Wrap once more around standing line on other side of previous wraps
  4. Pull tight

How It Works:

  • Slides along rope when pushed
  • Grips tight under tension
  • Allows easy adjustment
TRUCKER'S HITCH (Mechanical Advantage)

Use: Securing loads, creating very tight lines, tarps

How to Tie:

  1. Tie one end to anchor point
  2. Create loop in standing line (slip knot or alpine butterfly)
  3. Pass working end around second anchor point
  4. Thread working end through loop
  5. Pull for 3:1 mechanical advantage
  6. Secure with two half hitches

Why It's Powerful:

  • Provides 3:1 pulley system
  • Can create extremely tight lines
  • Essential for securing tarps in wind
FIGURE-EIGHT KNOT (Stopper Knot)

Use: Prevents rope from slipping through holes, climbing safety

How to Tie:

  1. Make loop in rope
  2. Pass working end around behind standing line
  3. Thread working end through loop
  4. Creates figure-8 shape
  5. Tighten

Why Use It:

  • Won't slip through holes or pulleys
  • Easy to inspect for correctness
  • Strong and reliable
  • Doesn't jam as tight as overhand knot
TWO HALF HITCHES (Finishing Knot)

Use: Finishing other knots, securing rope to trees/posts

How to Tie:

  1. Wrap rope around object
  2. Wrap working end around standing line and through loop (first half hitch)
  3. Repeat above step (second half hitch)
  4. Tighten both against object

Common Uses:

  • Finishing trucker's hitch
  • Tying rope to tree
  • Securing anything to a post

SQUARE LASHING (90¬∞ Connections)

Use: Connecting poles at right angles

Procedure:

  1. Start with clove hitch on vertical pole
  2. Wrap rope over horizontal pole, under vertical pole
  3. Continue wrapping in square pattern 3-4 times
  4. Wrap 3-4 times perpendicular to first wraps (frapping - tightens lashing)
  5. Finish with clove hitch on horizontal pole

Uses:

  • Building shelter frames
  • Creating rafts
  • Making tables/platforms
  • Any 90¬∞ joint
DIAGONAL LASHING (Preventing Racking)

Use: Preventing scissoring motion in crossed poles

Procedure:

  1. Start with timber hitch around both poles
  2. Wrap diagonally 3-4 times in one direction
  3. Wrap diagonally 3-4 times in other direction
  4. Add frapping turns between poles
  5. Finish with clove hitch
SHEAR LASHING (Parallel Poles)

Use: Joining parallel poles that will spread apart

Procedure:

  1. Lay poles parallel
  2. Start with clove hitch on one pole
  3. Wrap loosely around both poles 7-8 times
  4. Add frapping turns between poles
  5. Finish with clove hitch
  6. Spread poles to create tripod or A-frame

Uses:

  • Tripods for cooking
  • A-frame structures
  • Shelter supports

REVERSE WRAP TECHNIQUE (Making Strong Rope)

Step-by-Step:

  1. Start: Take bundle of fibers, fold in half
  2. Split: Divide into two roughly equal sections
  3. Twist one section: Twist away from you (clockwise if right-handed)
  4. Wrap over other section: Bring twisted section over and toward you
  5. Repeat: Now twist the section that's on top, wrap over the other
  6. Add fibers: When getting thin, lay new fibers alongside and incorporate
  7. Continue: Keep consistent twist and wrap rhythm

Why It Works:

  • Opposing twists lock together
  • Can't unravel
  • Gets stronger as you pull it
  • Can make any length

Tips:

  • Keep tension consistent
  • Slightly damp fibers work better
  • Overlap new fibers by 6+ inches
  • Practice makes perfect - first attempts will be rough

SUN NAVIGATION

Basic Sun Facts:

  • Sets in WEST (general direction)
  • At solar noon: Sun is at highest point and casts north-south shadow
  • Northern Hemisphere: Sun is to the south at noon

SHADOW STICK METHOD (Accurate Direction Finding)
  1. Place vertical stick in level ground (3+ feet tall works best)
  2. Mark tip of shadow with rock/stick
  3. Wait 15-30 minutes
  4. Mark new shadow tip position
  5. Draw line between the two marks
  6. Result: Line runs WEST (first mark) to EAST (second mark)
  7. Draw perpendicular line for North-South

Why it works: Sun moves east to west, so shadow moves west to east

ANALOG WATCH METHOD

Northern Hemisphere:

  1. Hold watch horizontal (face up)
  2. Point hour hand at the sun
  3. Find midpoint between hour hand and 12 o'clock
  4. That line points SOUTH

Southern Hemisphere:

  1. Hold watch horizontal
  2. Point 12 o'clock at the sun
  3. Find midpoint between 12 and hour hand
  4. That line points NORTH

Note: Less accurate than shadow stick but faster. During Daylight Saving Time, use 1 o'clock instead of 12.

STAR NAVIGATION

Northern Hemisphere - Finding North Star (Polaris):

  1. Locate Big Dipper constellation (looks like pot with handle)
  2. Find the two stars that form front edge of "pot" (pointer stars)
  3. Draw imaginary line through these stars
  4. Extend line about 5x the distance between pointer stars
  5. This leads to North Star (Polaris)
  6. North Star is ALWAYS due north and doesn't move

Southern Hemisphere - Using Southern Cross:

  1. Find Southern Cross constellation (4 bright stars forming cross)
  2. Identify long axis of the cross
  3. Extend long axis 4.5 times its length
  4. Drop imaginary line straight down to horizon
  5. That point on horizon is SOUTH

  • Three flashes of light
  • Three smoke columns

    • 50-100 feet apart

    • Visible for many miles

    2. Make V with fingers of other hand

    4. Reflect sunlight from mirror onto palm with V

    • Phone screen

    • Polished aluminum can bottom

Essential Knots for Survival

ü™¢ Why Knots Matter

Proper knots can save your life - they secure shelters, create tools, aid in rescue, and enable countless survival tasks. Learn these knots until you can tie them in the dark, with cold hands, under stress.

KNOT TERMINOLOGY (Know These Terms)
  • Standing Line: Main part of rope (not the working end)
  • Working End: End you're tying with
  • Bight: Bend in rope without crossing over itself (U-shape)
  • Loop: Rope crossed over itself (O-shape)
  • Tag End: Short end remaining after tying knot
BOWLINE - "King of Knots" (Rescue Knot)

Use: Creates non-slip loop, easy to untie after loading, rescue situations

How to Tie:

  1. Make small loop in standing line (rabbit hole)
  2. Pass working end up through loop (rabbit comes out of hole)
  3. Wrap working end behind standing line (around tree)
  4. Bring working end back down through original loop (back in hole)
  5. Tighten by pulling standing line and working end

Memory Aid:

"Rabbit comes out of hole, around the tree, back in the hole"

Uses:

  • Rescue loop to lower/raise person
  • Securing rope to tree or post
  • Creating fixed loop for anchoring
  • Tying around waist for safety line
SQUARE KNOT (Reef Knot)

Use: Joining two ropes of equal diameter, securing bandages

How to Tie:

  1. Right end over left, then under (first half)
  2. Left end over right, then under (second half)
  3. Pull tight

Memory Aid:

"Right over left, left over right"

‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING:

  • NOT for critical loads or climbing
  • Can slip if ropes different diameter
  • Can jam tight under heavy load
  • Good for first aid, light securing only
CLOVE HITCH

    Disaster-Specific Response Plans

    EARTHQUAKE SURVIVAL

    DURING Earthquake:

    1. DROP, COVER, HOLD ON - Most important rule!
      • Drop to hands and knees (prevents being knocked down)
      • Cover head and neck under desk/table
      • Hold on and move with it until shaking stops
    2. If no cover available: Curl against interior wall, cover head and neck
    3. STAY AWAY from: Windows, mirrors, hanging objects, tall furniture
    4. If outside: Move away from buildings, power lines, trees - get to open area
    5. If in car: Pull over safely and stop. Stay inside with seatbelt on. Avoid bridges and overpasses.
    6. If in bed: Stay there, cover head with pillow

    AFTER Earthquake:

    • Expect aftershocks: Can be as dangerous as main quake
    • Check for injuries: Provide first aid
    • Check for hazards:
      • Gas leaks - smell or hear hissing? Shut off main valve, evacuate, call authorities
      • Electrical damage - shut off at breaker if wiring damaged
      • Structural damage - if building unsafe, evacuate immediately
      • Chemical spills - evacuate and report
    • Don't use elevators - may be damaged or power could fail
    • Stay away from damaged buildings - further collapse possible
    • Check communications: Text may work when calls don't
    • Turn on radio: For emergency information and instructions

    ‚ö†Ô∏è How to Shut Off Gas (CRITICAL SKILL):

    1. Locate gas meter (usually outside near street)
    2. Find shut-off valve on inlet pipe
    3. Use adjustable wrench to turn valve 1/4 turn (perpendicular to pipe)
    4. Once off, DO NOT turn back on yourself - gas company must inspect first
    5. ONLY shut off if you smell/hear gas leak!
    FIRE EMERGENCY

    Fire Escape Plan (Prepare NOW):

    • Two escape routes from every room
    • Designated meeting point outside (specific spot across street)
    • Practice monthly with all family members
    • Escape ladders for upper floors
    • Know how to unlock/open all windows

    DURING Fire:

    1. Alert everyone: Yell "FIRE!" loud and clear
    2. Get out immediately: Don't gather belongings
    3. Stay low: Smoke and toxic gases rise - crawl under smoke
    4. Feel doors before opening:
      • Touch with back of hand (more sensitive)
      • If hot = fire on other side, use alternate route
      • If cool = open slowly, be ready to slam shut
    5. Close doors behind you: Slows fire spread
    6. If clothes catch fire: STOP, DROP, ROLL
      • Stop - don't run (fans flames)
      • Drop - to ground
      • Roll - covers flames, smothers fire
      • Cover face with hands while rolling
    7. If trapped in room:
      • Close door, stuff gaps with wet towels/clothes
      • Call 911 and report exact location
      • Signal at window with flashlight or bright cloth
      • If smoke enters, stay low near floor

    AFTER Escaping:

    • Meet at designated spot - account for everyone
    • Call 911 from safe location
    • NEVER re-enter burning building - for any reason!
    • Tell firefighters if anyone is unaccounted for
    FLOOD SURVIVAL

    BEFORE Flood (If Warning Given):

    • Move to higher ground immediately if evacuation ordered
    • Move valuables to upper floors
    • Turn off utilities if instructed
    • Fill bathtubs and containers with clean water (supply may be contaminated)
    • Bring outdoor furniture inside

    DURING Flood:

    • Move to higher ground immediately - don't wait
    • NEVER walk in moving water:
      • 6 inches can knock you down
      • 1 foot can sweep away vehicles
      • Can't see what's underneath (holes, debris, live wires)
    • NEVER drive through flooded roads:
      • "Turn Around, Don't Drown"
      • Most flood deaths occur in vehicles
      • Water depth is deceiving
      • 2 feet of water will float most vehicles
    • Avoid downed power lines - assume all are live
    • If trapped in building: Go to highest floor, attic if necessary
    • If trapped on roof: Signal for help, stay visible

    AFTER Flood:

    • Listen for all-clear before returning
    • Water safety:
      • Don't use tap water until declared safe
      • Boil water or use purification tablets
      • Floodwater is contaminated with sewage and chemicals
    • Food safety: Discard any food that contacted floodwater
    • Document damage: Photos for insurance before cleaning
    • Structural concerns: Have professionals inspect before occupying
    • Mold prevention: Dry out building within 24-48 hours
    TORNADO SURVIVAL

    Warning Signs:

    • Dark, greenish sky
    • Large, dark, low-lying clouds
    • Large hail
    • Loud roar (like freight train)
    • Visible rotating cloud/funnel

    DURING Tornado:

    1. Best location - Interior room on lowest floor:
      • Basement center or under stairs
      • No basement: interior hallway or closet away from windows
      • Bathroom with tub (sturdy plumbing provides extra protection)
    2. Protect head and neck:
      • Get under heavy furniture if possible
      • Cover with mattress, blankets, sleeping bag
      • Crouch low, head down, hands over head
    3. If in mobile home: EVACUATE to sturdy shelter immediately
    4. If outside with no shelter:
      • Lie flat in ditch or low area
      • Cover head and neck
      • DO NOT seek shelter under highway overpass (dangerous myth!)
    5. If in vehicle:
      • If you can see sturdy building nearby, drive to it
      • If not, park safely, stay belted, duck below windows, cover head
      • If possible, evacuate vehicle and lie flat in ditch

    ‚ö†Ô∏è DANGEROUS MYTHS:

    • Myth: "Open windows to equalize pressure"
      • FACT: Wastes time, doesn't help, tornado will blow out windows anyway
    • Myth: "Southwest corner of building is safest"
      • FACT: Interior room on lowest floor, regardless of corner
    • Myth: "Highway overpass is safe shelter"
      • FACT: Extremely dangerous - acts as wind tunnel, flying debris
    HURRICANE/TYPHOON PREPARATION

    BEFORE Hurricane (When Warning Issued):

    • Evacuate if ordered - authorities know best
    • If staying:
      • Board up windows with plywood (5/8 inch minimum)
      • Bring outdoor objects inside
      • Fill bathtubs and containers with water
      • Charge all devices
      • Fill vehicle gas tank
      • Get cash (ATMs won't work in power outage)
      • Stock up on essential supplies
    • Know your evacuation route and zone
    • Plan for pets - many shelters don't accept animals

    DURING Hurricane:

    • Stay indoors away from windows and glass
    • Interior room on lowest floor (not basement - flooding risk)
    • Beware the EYE:
      • Calm period in center of storm
      • Winds will resume from opposite direction
      • Often worse than first half
      • STAY INSIDE during eye!
    • Listen to battery radio for updates

    AFTER Hurricane:

    • Storm surge kills most victims - flooding after hurricane ends
    • Don't go out until all-clear given
    • Avoid floodwater and standing water
    • Watch for downed power lines
    • Use flashlights, not candles (gas leaks may be present)
    • Boil water until declared safe

      ‚ö†Ô∏è IMMEDIATE LIFE THREATS (First 3 Minutes)

      ABC Priority Check:

      • Airway: Is the airway clear and open?
      • Breathing: Is the person breathing normally?
      • Circulation: Is there severe bleeding? Can you feel a pulse?

      If ANY of these are compromised, you have a life-threatening emergency. Act immediately!

      SEVERE BLEEDING CONTROL
      1. Direct Pressure: Apply firm, continuous pressure directly on the wound with the cleanest cloth available. Hold for 10+ minutes without checking.
      2. Elevation: Raise the injured area above heart level (if no fracture suspected).
      3. Tourniquet: Only for life-threatening limb bleeding
        • Apply 2-3 inches above wound (never on a joint)
        • Tighten until bleeding stops completely
        • Note exact time applied (write on tourniquet or skin)
        • DO NOT REMOVE once applied
        • Get medical help immediately
      UNCONSCIOUS PERSON NOT BREATHING - CPR
      1. Call 911 immediately (or have someone else call)
      2. Position: Place person on firm, flat surface
      3. Hand Placement: Center of chest between nipples
      4. Compression Technique:
        • Heel of one hand, other hand on top
        • Fingers interlaced, arms straight
        • Push HARD and FAST - 2 inches deep
        • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (think "Stayin' Alive" beat)
      5. Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 breaths (if trained in rescue breathing)
      6. Hands-Only CPR: If untrained, continuous compressions without breaths is highly effective
      7. Continue until: Help arrives, person revives, or you're physically unable to continue
      CHOKING - HEIMLICH MANEUVER

      Conscious Adult:

      1. Ask "Are you choking?" (If they nod but can't speak - choking emergency)
      2. If they can cough forcefully, encourage continued coughing
      3. If they CANNOT cough, breathe, or speak:
        • Stand behind them
        • Make a fist just above navel, below ribcage
        • Grasp fist with other hand
        • Quick, upward thrusts into abdomen
        • Repeat until object is expelled

      Unconscious Person:

      1. Lower to ground carefully
      2. Begin CPR immediately
      3. Before giving rescue breaths, look in mouth for visible objects
      4. Remove only if clearly visible (don't do blind finger sweeps)

      Self-Heimlich:

      • Use your own fist for abdominal thrusts, OR
      • Lean over chair back, counter edge, or railing and thrust abdomen against it

      IF YOU ARE BEING THREATENED

      1. Create Distance: Move away immediately - distance is safety
      2. Move to Public Area: Head toward people, lights, cameras
      3. Use Loud Commands: Yell "BACK OFF!" "GET AWAY!" "STOP!" - Be loud and clear
      4. Call for Help: Dial 911 or emergency services
      5. Trust Your Instincts: If something feels wrong, it probably is - act immediately

      1. üß† SURVIVAL PSYCHOLOGY & MINDSET

      The 80/20 Rule of Survival

      80% Mental Strength - 20% Physical Skill

      Your mind is your most important survival tool. Physical skills and equipment matter, but mental fortitude determines whether you'll use them effectively. People with superior mental strength have survived with minimal resources, while those who gave up mentally perished despite having everything they needed.

      Core Mental Pillars

      1. ADAPTABILITY

      The ability to adjust when plans fail is crucial. Embrace "good enough" solutions over perfect ones. What you have is what you work with - flexibility beats perfection every time.

      2. RESILIENCE

      You are tougher than you think. Throughout history, humans have survived incredible odds. Small progress beats paralysis. Every setback is temporary.

      3. HOPE & PURPOSE

      Maintain your will to live by thinking of loved ones. Set small, achievable goals. Celebrate small victories. Hope is not a strategy, but it's essential fuel.

      4. ACTION ORIENTATION

      Movement beats paralysis. Imperfect action beats perfect inaction. Start with what you can control and build momentum with small wins.

      5. SITUATIONAL AWARENESS

      Constantly assess your environment. Notice changes and anomalies. Trust your instincts. Stay alert to both opportunities and threats.

      COMBAT PANIC & FEAR - Box Breathing Technique

      When panic strikes, regain control with this simple breathing exercise:

      1. Inhale slowly through nose for 4 counts
      2. Hold breath for 4 counts
      3. Exhale slowly through mouth for 4 counts
      4. Hold empty for 4 counts
      5. Repeat until you feel calm and in control

      This technique is used by military personnel, first responders, and athletes to manage stress under extreme pressure.

      Decision-Making Under Stress

      • Slow Down: Take time to think when possible - hasty decisions kill
      • Keep It Simple: Complex plans fail under stress
      • One Thing at a Time: Focus only on the immediate next step
      • Talk It Out: Speaking out loud helps organize thoughts and catches errors

      ‚ö†Ô∏è COMMON PSYCHOLOGICAL KILLERS

      1. GIVING UP TOO SOON

      Most rescues happen within 72 hours. Help is often much closer than you think. Your situation may suddenly improve. People who survived reported being on the verge of giving up moments before rescue.

      2. PANIC-INDUCED POOR DECISIONS

      Panic makes you run when you should stay. Panic makes you stay when you should move. Panic wastes precious energy and resources. Use STOP protocol immediately.

      3. ISOLATION & LONELINESS

      Counter this by talking out loud to yourself, creating routines, journaling if possible, and actively remembering loved ones in detail.

      4. DESPAIR FROM LACK OF PROGRESS

      Set smaller, achievable goals. Measure progress differently. Celebrate any forward movement. Remember: survival is rarely linear - setbacks are normal.

      2. ‚ö° PRIORITY ASSESSMENT & IMMEDIATE ACTIONS

      When ANY Emergency Strikes - Use STOP Protocol

      This is your first and most important action in ANY survival situation. It prevents panic-driven mistakes that make situations worse.

      THE STOP PROTOCOL (Your First Response)

      SSIT

      • Literally sit down if it's safe to do so
      • Stop all panic-driven movement immediately
      • Prevent making the situation worse
      • Take control of your actions before proceeding

      TTHINK

      • What exactly happened? How did I get here?
      • What are the immediate threats to my life?
      • What resources and supplies do I have?
      • Does anyone know where I am? When will they notice I'm missing?

      OOBSERVE

      • Survey your environment completely - 360¬∞ awareness
      • Inventory ALL supplies, tools, and materials available
      • Assess your physical condition honestly - injuries, fatigue, hunger
      • Note weather conditions, time of day, and terrain

      PPLAN

      • Prioritize needs using Rule of Threes
      • Create specific, actionable steps
      • Identify backup plans for each step
      • Set reasonable timeline for decisions and actions

      Rule of Threes - Survival Priority Timeline

      3 SECONDS - Hope & Will to Live

      Before anything else, you must decide to survive. This mental commitment is the foundation of everything that follows. Without the will to live, even perfect resources won't save you.

      3 MINUTES - Air & Severe Bleeding

      Immediate life threats that kill fastest:

      • Blocked airway (choking, drowning, throat swelling)
      • Severe bleeding (arterial or major vessel)
      • Cardiac arrest (heart stopped)
      • Anaphylactic shock (severe allergic reaction)
      • Severe head trauma

      Action: Address these IMMEDIATELY - nothing else matters if these aren't handled first.

      3 HOURS - Shelter in Harsh Conditions

      Environmental threats:

      • Extreme cold (hypothermia onset)
      • Extreme heat (heat stroke risk)
      • Severe weather (storms, high winds)
      • Wet conditions combined with cold

      Action: In harsh weather, shelter becomes urgent. Exposure kills quickly.

      3 DAYS - Water

      Dehydration timeline:

      • Day 1: Thirst, reduced mental and physical performance
      • Day 2: Significant cognitive impairment, weakness, nausea
      • Day 3: Life-threatening - organ failure begins

      Action: Find and purify water source. Never drink unpurified water if you can avoid it.

      3 WEEKS - Food

      Starvation timeline:

      • Week 1: Hunger, some weakness, body uses glycogen stores
      • Week 2: Noticeable energy loss, body starts consuming muscle
      • Week 3: Serious health impacts, severe weakness

      Action: You can survive weeks without food. Don't risk injury or waste energy on food if other priorities aren't met.

      3 MONTHS - Human Contact & Companionship

      Psychological needs for long-term survival:

      • Isolation effects on mental health
      • Hope and motivation maintenance
      • Purpose and meaning

      Action: Create routines, talk to yourself, journal, stay mentally engaged.

      Immediate Action Sequence

      STEP 1: SECURE IMMEDIATE SAFETY (First 60 Seconds)
      • Move away from immediate danger (fire, traffic, attackers, falling debris)
      • Quickly check yourself for injuries - especially bleeding
      • Ensure you can breathe freely
      • Stop any severe bleeding immediately
      STEP 2: ASSESS SITUATION (Next 5 Minutes)
      • Apply full STOP protocol
      • Determine exact location if possible (landmarks, GPS if working)
      • Check all communications (phone, radio, emergency beacon)
      • Complete inventory of supplies, tools, and resources
      • Note time, weather, and environmental conditions
      STEP 3: ESTABLISH PRIORITIES (Next 15 Minutes)
      • Address any remaining medical needs
      • Secure or create shelter if weather is threatening
      • Locate or plan water source acquisition
      • Prepare signaling devices/methods for rescue
      • Create backup plans for each priority
      STEP 4: TAKE ACTION (Ongoing)
      • Execute plan systematically - one step at a time
      • Monitor conditions and reassess regularly (every hour initially)
      • Adapt quickly to changing conditions
      • Maintain positive mental attitude and energy conservation

      GREEN DELAYED
      (Minor)      		 Small cuts, bruises, comfort issues, minor pain, non-essential tasks Address when time and resources allow
      BLACK EXPECTANT

      3. üè• MEDICAL EMERGENCIES & FIRST AID

      ‚ö†Ô∏è WHEN TO CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY

      • Unconsciousness or altered mental state
      • Difficulty breathing or no breathing
      • Severe bleeding that won't stop
      • Chest pain or pressure lasting more than a few minutes
      • Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis)
      • Signs of stroke (facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty)
      • Severe burns or chemical exposure
      • Suspected poisoning or overdose
      • Severe head, neck, or back injury
      • Choking that can't be cleared

      The ABCs of Emergency Care

      A - AIRWAY

      Check if airway is open and clear:

      • Look: Watch chest for rising and falling
      • Listen: Put ear near mouth/nose for breathing sounds
      • Feel: Feel for breath on your cheek

      If airway is blocked:

      • Tilt head back, lift chin (unless neck injury suspected)
      • Look for visible obstructions in mouth
      • Remove visible objects with finger sweep (don't blind sweep)
      • If unconscious and choking, begin CPR
      B - BREATHING

      If person is not breathing:

      • Begin rescue breathing: 1 breath every 5-6 seconds
      • Watch for chest to rise with each breath
      • If chest doesn't rise, reposition airway and try again
      • If no pulse detected, begin full CPR immediately

      Signs of breathing difficulty:

      • Gasping or wheezing sounds
      • Blue lips or fingernails (cyanosis)
      • Confusion or agitation
      • Inability to speak in full sentences
      C - CIRCULATION

      Check for pulse and severe bleeding:

      • Pulse points: Carotid artery (neck) or radial artery (wrist)
      • Check for 10 seconds before determining no pulse
      • Control any severe bleeding immediately with direct pressure
      • Treat for shock if signs present

      Pulse rates (beats per minute):

      • Adults: 60-100 (normal), <60 or >100 (concerning)
      • Children: 70-120 (normal)
      • Infants: 100-160 (normal)

      Severe Bleeding Control - Detailed Techniques

      METHOD 1: DIRECT PRESSURE (Always Try First)
      1. Use cleanest material available: Gauze, clean cloth, shirt, towel - even your gloved hand if nothing else
      2. Apply firm, continuous pressure directly on wound - press hard!
      3. Hold for minimum 10 minutes without peeking or releasing
      4. If bleeding through: Add more material on top (don't remove first layer)
      5. Bandage in place when bleeding stops - maintain pressure with wrap

      ‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Most people don't press hard enough or long enough. You need to press HARD and maintain it.

      METHOD 2: ELEVATION (Combined with Pressure)
      • Raise wounded area above heart level whenever possible
      • Uses gravity to reduce blood flow to injury
      • Only if no fracture or spine injury suspected
      • Always combine with direct pressure - not standalone treatment
      METHOD 3: PRESSURE POINTS (When Direct Pressure Insufficient)

      For Arm Bleeding - Brachial Artery:

      • Located on inside of upper arm, between bicep and tricep
      • Press artery firmly against arm bone (humerus)
      • Use heel of hand with firm pressure
      • Maintain while also using direct pressure on wound

      For Leg Bleeding - Femoral Artery:

      • Located in groin area, where leg meets torso
      • Press firmly against pelvic bone
      • Use for severe thigh bleeding above knee
      • Very effective but requires significant pressure
      METHOD 4: TOURNIQUET (Life-Threatening Limb Bleeding ONLY)

      When to Use a Tourniquet:

      • Severe arterial bleeding from arm or leg
      • Direct pressure has failed or is impossible
      • Multiple casualties requiring triage
      • Traumatic amputation

      How to Apply Properly:

      1. Location: 2-3 inches above wound, never on a joint
      2. Material: Use commercial tourniquet if available, or 2-3 inch wide cloth/belt
      3. Tightness: Tighten until bleeding COMPLETELY stops - this will be very tight
      4. Time: Write exact time applied on tourniquet or patient's forehead
      5. Never remove: Once applied, only medical professionals should remove
      6. Mark patient: Write "TQ" and time on forehead if possible

      ‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL WARNINGS:

      • Limb may be lost, but life is saved - acceptable trade-off in life-threatening bleeding
      • Never use thin materials (wire, rope, cord) - causes tissue damage
      • Don't loosen to "check" - keep it tight until medical help arrives
      • Can safely remain in place for 2+ hours
      • Get professional medical attention immediately

      CPR - Complete Guide

      ADULT CPR (Detailed Step-by-Step)

      When to Start CPR:

      • Person is unconscious
      • Not breathing or only gasping
      • No pulse detected (check for 10 seconds)

      Procedure:

      1. Call for help: Dial 911 immediately (or have someone else call). Get AED if available.
      2. Position patient: Place on firm, flat surface (floor, not bed)
      3. Position yourself: Kneel beside patient's chest
      4. Hand placement:
        • Locate center of chest between nipples
        • Place heel of one hand on center of chest
        • Place other hand on top, interlace fingers
        • Keep fingers off chest (only heel of hand contacts)
      5. Body position:
        • Position shoulders directly over hands
        • Keep arms straight, elbows locked
        • Use body weight, not arm strength
      6. Compression technique:
        • Push HARD and FAST - compress at least 2 inches deep (but no more than 2.4 inches)
        • Allow complete chest recoil between compressions
        • Don't lean on chest between compressions
        • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (think "Stayin' Alive" by Bee Gees)
      7. Rescue breaths (if trained):
        • After 30 compressions, tilt head back, lift chin
        • Pinch nose shut
        • Make complete seal over person's mouth
        • Give 2 breaths (1 second each, watch for chest rise)
        • Return to compressions immediately
      8. Continue: 30 compressions : 2 breaths ratio until:
        • Emergency help arrives and takes over
        • Person starts breathing normally
        • You are too exhausted to continue
        • Scene becomes unsafe

      HANDS-ONLY CPR (For Untrained or Uncomfortable with Rescue Breaths)

      • Continuous chest compressions ONLY - no rescue breaths
      • Same hand placement and compression technique
      • 100-120 compressions per minute without stopping
      • Research shows this is highly effective and better than no CPR!
      • Recommended for witnessed cardiac arrest in adults

      Shock - Recognition and Treatment

      SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS OF SHOCK
      • Pale, cold, clammy skin (feels moist and cool)
      • Rapid, weak pulse (>100 bpm, hard to feel)
      • Rapid, shallow breathing (fast and ineffective)
      • Confusion, anxiety, or altered mental state
      • Weakness, dizziness, or feeling faint
      • Nausea or vomiting
      • Dilated pupils
      • Extreme thirst
      SHOCK TREATMENT PROTOCOL
      1. Lay person down (unless head, neck, or chest injury - then keep sitting if breathing easier)
      2. Elevate legs 12 inches (unless leg/spine injury suspected) - helps blood return to vital organs
      3. Keep warm with blanket above and below person - maintain body temperature
      4. Monitor breathing and pulse constantly - be ready to start CPR
      5. Reassure calmly - reduce anxiety which worsens shock
      6. DO NOT give food or water (risk of vomiting and aspiration)
      7. Get emergency medical help immediately - shock is life-threatening

      Fractures and Broken Bones

      HOW TO IDENTIFY A FRACTURE
      • Deformity: Unnatural angle or position of limb
      • Swelling: Rapid swelling at injury site
      • Bruising: May appear immediately or develop over hours
      • Severe pain: Especially with movement or pressure
      • Loss of function: Cannot use limb normally or bear weight
      • Bone visible: Open fracture - bone protruding through skin
      • Grinding sensation: Crepitus - bone fragments rubbing
      • Numbness/tingling: Below injury - nerve damage concern

      ‚ö†Ô∏è FRACTURE TREATMENT PRINCIPLES

      1. DO NOT MOVE if neck/spine injury suspected - call for help and stabilize in place
      2. DO NOT try to realign broken bones - causes further damage and excruciating pain
      3. Immobilize above and below injury - stabilize joints on both sides of fracture
      4. Splint in position found - don't straighten or reposition
      5. Check circulation regularly - below injury site (pulse, color, warmth, sensation)
      6. Treat for shock - fractures can cause shock, especially femur fractures
      7. Get professional medical help - all fractures need proper treatment
      IMPROVISED SPLINTING MATERIALS

      Rigid Materials (for splint structure):

      • Sticks and branches (straight and strong)
      • Rolled newspapers or magazines
      • Cardboard (multiple layers)
      • Tent poles or trekking poles
      • Pieces of wood or plastic
      • SAM splint (if in first aid kit)

      Padding Materials (for comfort and protection):

      • Cloth, clothing, towels
      • Blankets or sleeping bags
      • Foam pads or camping mats
      • Bubble wrap or newspapers

      Securing Materials (to hold splint in place):

      • Cloth strips or torn clothing
      • Belts or straps
      • Medical tape or duct tape
      • Rope or paracord
      • Bandages or elastic wraps

      Specific Fracture Management

      Fracture Location Splinting Technique Special Considerations
      Arm/Wrist Splint from elbow to fingertips. Use sling for support. Bind arm to body for stability. Check fingers for circulation every 30 minutes. Elevate to reduce swelling.
      Leg/Ankle Splint full length from hip to below ankle. Can use other leg as splint if needed. Never attempt to bear weight. Pad between legs if tying together.
      Femur (Thigh) Full leg splint, hip to ankle. May require traction splint. VERY serious - causes severe blood loss and shock. Get help fast!
      Collarbone Sling and swathe (bind arm to chest). Common injury. Usually heals well with rest.
      Ribs DO NOT wrap or bind tightly. Support with arm sling if needed. Binding prevents deep breathing and can cause pneumonia.

      OPEN FRACTURES (Bone Breaking Through Skin)

      This is a medical emergency with high infection risk!

      1. DO NOT push bone back into wound
      2. Cover wound with clean, moist dressing (sterile if possible)
      3. Splint carefully in position found
      4. Monitor circulation below injury constantly
      5. Treat for shock - likely to develop
      6. Get emergency medical help immediately - surgery likely needed
      7. Start antibiotics if available and evacuation will be delayed

      Water - Finding, Purifying, and Storing

      üíß Water is Your #1 Priority After Safety

      The human body can survive 3 weeks without food, but only 3 days without water. In hot conditions or with physical exertion, this timeline shrinks to as little as 24 hours. Finding and purifying water must be a top priority.

      WATER LOCATION STRATEGIES

      Natural Indicators of Water:

      • Animal tracks converging and leading downhill
      • Green vegetation in otherwise arid areas
      • Bird flight patterns - flying low in morning/evening toward water
      • Insect concentrations - especially bees and mosquitoes
      • Sound of running water - can carry for miles in quiet areas
      • Valley bottoms - water flows downhill and collects
      • Rock formations - water can collect in depressions

      Water Sources Ranked (Best to Worst):

      1. Running streams - Moving water, less contamination
      2. Springs - Water emerging from ground, often clean
      3. Rain collection - Pure when first falling
      4. Lakes and ponds - Still water, more contamination
      5. Swamps and marshes - Last resort, heavy purification needed

      Water Collection Methods

      DEW COLLECTION
      • Use absorbent cloth (shirt, towel, bandana) to wipe dew from grass
      • Wring collected water into container
      • Best results in early morning before sunrise
      • Can collect 1-2 cups per hour in good conditions
      • Relatively clean water source
      RAIN COLLECTION
      • Use ANY container - pots, bottles, hollowed logs
      • Create funnel with tarp weighted in center
      • Let first few minutes of rain wash contaminants from collection surface
      • Can collect large amounts quickly in heavy rain
      • Cleanest if falling directly into container (no ground contact)
      SOLAR STILL (Emergency Method)

      Construction:

      1. Dig hole 3 feet across, 2 feet deep in sunny location
      2. Place collection container in center bottom
      3. Add vegetation around container (increases water production)
      4. Cover hole with clear plastic sheet
      5. Weight center of plastic over container with small rock
      6. Seal edges completely with dirt/sand

      How it works: Sun heats ground and vegetation, water evaporates, condenses on plastic, drips into container

      Yield: 1-2 cups per day in good conditions

      Limitation: Requires clear plastic and sunny weather. Labor intensive for low yield.

      TRANSPIRATION BAGS
      • Tie clear plastic bag tightly around leafy branch (still on living tree)
      • Position bag so water collects in corner
      • Place small rock in corner to create collection point
      • Avoid toxic plants (test if uncertain)
      • Can produce 1-2 cups per day per bag
      • Use multiple bags for better yield
      • Water is usually clean enough to drink without purification
      PLANT WATER SOURCES
      Plant Type Method Yield
      Vines Cut high first, then low. Let drip into mouth. Avoid milky sap. Variable
      Banana Trees Cut tree down, scoop water from stump. 1-2 cups
      Bamboo Shake to hear water sound, then drill/cut to access. Variable
      Coconuts Green coconuts contain fresh water. Crack open carefully. 1-2 cups per coconut

      ‚ö†Ô∏è WATER PURIFICATION - CRITICAL IMPORTANCE

      NEVER drink untreated water from natural sources!

      Waterborne pathogens can cause:

      • Giardia - severe diarrhea and dehydration
      • Cryptosporidium - life-threatening in weakened state
      • E. coli and other bacteria - serious illness
      • Parasites - long-term health problems

      In survival situations, diarrhea from contaminated water can kill you faster than dehydration!

      Water Purification Methods

      METHOD 1: BOILING (Most Reliable)
      • Rolling boil for 1 minute at sea level
      • Rolling boil for 3 minutes above 6,500 feet elevation
      • Kills ALL harmful organisms - bacteria, viruses, parasites
      • Let cool before drinking (hot water increases dehydration)
      • Only downside: requires fire/fuel and time
      • This is the gold standard when possible
      METHOD 2: CHEMICAL TREATMENT
      Chemical Dosage Wait Time Notes
      Purification Tablets Follow package instructions 30-60 minutes Most effective. Expensive but lightweight.
      Iodine 2-4 drops per quart 30 minutes Bad taste. Not for pregnant women or long-term use.
      Bleach 2 drops per quart
      (unscented only)      		 30 minutes Use regular unscented bleach (5-6% sodium hypochlorite).
      METHOD 3: UV TREATMENT
      • SteriPEN devices: Battery powered, 90 seconds per liter
      • SODIS method (Solar Disinfection):
        • Fill clear plastic bottle with water
        • Place in direct sunlight for 6 hours (or 2 days if cloudy)
        • UV rays kill microorganisms
        • Free but slow and weather-dependent
      METHOD 4: FILTRATION
      • Commercial filters: Sawyer, LifeStraw, Katadyn - follow manufacturer instructions
      • Improvised filter (better than nothing but STILL PURIFY after):

      Building an Improvised Filter:

      Layer materials from bottom up in container with hole in bottom:

      1. Clean cloth or coffee filter (bottom)
      2. Fine sand - 2-3 inches
      3. Crushed charcoal from fire - 2-3 inches
      4. Coarse sand or gravel - 2-3 inches
      5. Larger rocks (top)

      Pour water through multiple times. This removes particles and some pathogens but is NOT sufficient alone. You MUST still boil or chemically treat filtered water!

      Shelter - Protection from Elements

      üè† Why Shelter is Critical

      Exposure kills faster than hunger or thirst in harsh conditions. Hypothermia can set in within 3 hours in cold, wet weather. Proper shelter maintains your core body temperature and protects you from wind, rain, sun, and snow.

      SHELTER SITE SELECTION - Critical Factors

      Safety First:

      • Avoid hazards: Dead trees (widowmakers), rockfall zones, avalanche paths
      • Check above: Look up for hanging branches, loose rocks
      • Flood risk: Stay above high water marks, not in dry creek beds
      • Animal danger: No bear trails, wasp nests, or animal dens nearby

      Environmental Protection:

      • Wind protection: Natural windbreaks (hills, boulders, dense trees)
      • Drainage: Slight slope away from shelter, not in depression
      • Insulation: Can you get off cold ground? Materials available?
      • Resources: Near water (not directly on it), firewood, building materials

      Rescue Consideration:

      • If you want to be found: Open area, visible from air and ground
      • If hiding: Concealed location, natural cover

      Emergency Shelter Types

      DEBRIS HUT (Best for Solo Cold Weather Survival)

      Why it works: Small interior space traps body heat. Thick debris provides excellent insulation.

      Construction Steps:

      1. Find strong ridgepole 9-12 feet long
      2. Prop one end 3 feet high (on stump, rock, or forked tree)
      3. Lean branches at 45¬∞ angle on both sides of ridgepole
      4. Pile debris 3+ feet thick on all sides (leaves, pine needles, grass, bark)
      5. Create small entrance that you can plug with debris
      6. Stuff interior with dry insulation material
      7. Make just big enough for your body - smaller is warmer

      Key Points:

      • Interior should be barely bigger than your body
      • Thicker debris = better insulation (minimum 3 feet)
      • Dry materials only - wet insulation is useless
      • Can keep you alive in freezing temperatures with no fire
      LEAN-TO (Quick and Simple)

      Why it works: Fast to build. Good with reflector fire in front.

      Construction:

      1. Find fallen tree or create frame between two trees
      2. Lean branches at 45¬∞ angle against horizontal support
      3. Layer with bark, leaves, pine boughs - shingle style (bottom to top)
      4. Build reflector fire 4-6 feet in front of opening

      Limitations:

      • Open front exposes you to wind and cold
      • Requires fire for warmth
      • Not ideal for severe weather
      • Good for mild conditions or temporary shelter
      A-FRAME SHELTER (Better Wind Protection)

      Why it works: Enclosed on two sides. Better insulation than lean-to.

      Construction:

      1. Place ridgepole between two trees (or one end on ground)
      2. Lean branches at 45¬∞ on BOTH sides
      3. Cover completely with available materials
      4. Block one end completely
      5. Create small entrance at other end
      SNOW CAVE (Winter Survival)

      Why it works: Snow is excellent insulator. Inside stays around 32¬∞F even when outside is -40¬∞F.

      Construction:

      1. Find deep snow drift or dig into slope
      2. Tunnel horizontally 4-6 feet, then angle upward
      3. Hollow out sleeping chamber ABOVE entrance tunnel
      4. Make sleeping platform higher than entrance (cold air sinks)
      5. CRITICAL: Poke ventilation hole through roof (prevent CO‚ÇÇ poisoning)
      6. Smooth interior walls to prevent dripping
      7. Mark entrance clearly (can get buried in snowfall)

      ‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Warnings:

      • MUST have ventilation hole - check it regularly
      • Keep shovel inside to dig out if entrance gets buried
      • Don't make too large - harder to heat with body warmth
      • Check roof thickness (need 1+ foot for strength)

      üí° Insulation Principles - The Science of Staying Warm

      Ground Heat Loss (Biggest threat):

      • 60-80% of body heat lost to ground through conduction
      • ALWAYS insulate from ground - this is more important than roof
      • Use: pine boughs, dry leaves, grass, foam pads, emergency blankets
      • Minimum 6 inches thick, more is better

      Air Gap Insulation:

      • Trapped air provides insulation (like down jackets)
      • Multiple thin layers better than one thick layer
      • Fluffy materials (leaves, pine needles) trap more air

      Moisture is the Enemy:

      • Wet materials conduct heat away 25x faster than dry
      • Use only dry insulation materials
      • Keep sleeping area dry at all costs
      • Avoid sweating inside shelter

      Fire - Heat, Light, Signaling, Water Purification, Cooking

      üî• Why Fire is Essential

      • Warmth: Prevents hypothermia, dries wet clothing
      • Water purification: Boiling kills all pathogens
      • Cooking: Makes food safe and more digestible
      • Signaling: Visible for miles, especially smoke during day
      • Psychological: Massive morale boost, reduces fear
      • Predator deterrent: Most animals fear fire
      • Light: Extends useful hours, navigation at night

      Element Description Examples
      HEAT Ignition source to start fire Lighter, matches, ferro rod spark, friction, magnifying glass
      FUEL Combustible material Tinder ‚Üí Kindling ‚Üí Fuel wood (progressively larger)
      OXYGEN Air for combustion Proper airflow, don't smother, blow gently when needed

      Remove any one element and fire goes out. Control these three factors to control your fire.

      Fire Materials - Gathering and Preparation

      TINDER (Takes Spark Easily - Finger Diameter or Smaller)

      Purpose: Catches initial spark/flame and burns quickly to ignite kindling

      Natural Tinder:

      • Dry grass: Excellent, readily available
      • Birch bark: Contains oils, burns even when damp
      • Cedar bark: Shred into fine fibers, very flammable
      • Pine needles (dry): Good, but need to be very dry
      • Cattail fluff: Takes spark easily
      • Dead pine branches (inner): Fine, dry wood
      • Thistle down: Fluffy and catches spark well

      Man-Made Tinder (Carry These):

      • Cotton balls with petroleum jelly: Burns 3-5 minutes, waterproof in container
      • Dryer lint: Free, lightweight, very effective
      • Char cloth: Catches spark extremely well
      • Commercial fire starters: Reliable but takes space
      • Duct tape: Burns well when shredded

      Making "Feather Sticks":

      Cut thin curls in dry wood stick without detaching them completely. Creates excellent tinder from available wood.

      KINDLING (Finger to Thumb Thickness)

      Purpose: Bridges gap between tinder and fuel wood

      • Small dry twigs and branches
      • Split pieces of larger wood (inside is drier)
      • Fatwood (pine wood with high resin content - excellent)
      • Dead branches still on tree (drier than ground wood)
      • Pencil to marker thickness

      Tip: Look UP for dry wood. Dead branches still attached to trees haven't absorbed ground moisture.

      FUEL WOOD (Wrist Thickness and Larger)

      Purpose: Sustains fire long-term, creates hot coals for cooking

      Best Wood Types:

      • Hardwoods (Oak, Maple, Ash): Burn hot and long, best for sustained fire
      • Softwoods (Pine, Spruce, Fir): Ignite easily, burn fast, good for starting

      Finding Dry Wood:

      • Standing dead wood: Best source - hasn't absorbed ground moisture
      • Split large pieces: Inside is drier even if outside is wet
      • Under natural shelter: Rock overhangs, dense evergreen canopy
      • Avoid ground wood: Usually damp from ground contact

      üí° Wood Collection Pro Tips

      • Snap test: Dry wood snaps cleanly. Wet wood bends.
      • Gather 3x more than you think you need: You'll use it faster than expected
      • Prepare in stages: Sort into tinder, kindling, and fuel piles before starting
      • Protect from rain: Stack under shelter or cover with tarp
      • Size variety: Gradual progression from thin to thick works best

      Fire Starting Methods

      MODERN METHODS (Use These If Available)

      1. Lighter (Best Option):

      • Most reliable and efficient
      • Carry multiple - cheap insurance
      • Store in waterproof container
      • BIC lighters are most reliable

      2. Waterproof Matches:

      • Strike-anywhere matches are best
      • Store in waterproof container with striker
      • Practice using in wind and rain

      3. Ferro Rod (Ferrocerium Rod):

      • Works when wet, thousands of strikes
      • Creates 3000¬∞F sparks
      • Requires good tinder and technique
      • Practice before you need it!

      4. Magnifying Glass (Sunny Days Only):

      • Focus sun to pinpoint on tinder
      • Hold steady until smoke appears
      • Gently blow to create flame
      • Can use reading glasses, camera lens, clear water bottle
      PRIMITIVE METHOD: BOW DRILL (Most Reliable Friction Fire)

      Components Needed:

      1. Fireboard (Base):

      • Flat piece of softwood (cedar, cottonwood, willow)
      • About 12 inches long, 2 inches wide, 3/4 inch thick
      • Must be completely dry

      2. Spindle (Drill):

      • Straight hardwood stick
      • 3/4 inch diameter, 8-10 inches long
      • Rounded on both ends

      3. Bow:

      • Curved branch about arm's length
      • Strong and flexible (not too bendy)
      • Cordage (shoelace, paracord, plant fiber)

      4. Handhold (Top Piece):

      • Piece of hardwood or stone
      • Socket carved in bottom to hold spindle top
      • Grease socket with natural oils (ear wax, nose oil, pine resin)

      Procedure:

      1. Cut V-shaped notch in fireboard edge where spindle will spin
      2. Place tinder bundle under notch (bark platform underneath)
      3. Loop bowstring once around spindle
      4. Place spindle in fireboard depression, top in handhold socket
      5. Apply downward pressure with handhold
      6. Saw bow back and forth rapidly - fast rhythm!
      7. Dark powder/dust will form and fall into tinder bundle
      8. Continue until smoke streams and ember forms (glowing dust)
      9. Carefully transfer ember to tinder bundle
      10. Cup bundle gently and blow softly until it bursts into flame

      Troubleshooting:

      • Not enough smoke: Bow faster, press harder, check if wood is dry
      • Spindle slipping: Tighten bowstring, roughen spindle surface
      • Handhold smoking: Add more lubricant to socket
      • Ember won't ignite tinder: Tinder too coarse - needs finer material

      Steel Wool + Battery:

      • Stretch fine steel wool
      • Touch both battery terminals (9V works best, but AA works too)
      • Steel wool ignites instantly
      • Have tinder ready immediately

      Gum Wrapper + Battery:

      • Cut gum wrapper into hourglass shape (narrow center)
      • Touch ends to AA battery terminals
      • Center will ignite
      • Have tinder touching center when you connect

      Fire From Ice (Extreme Cold):

      • Carve clear ice into lens shape
      • Polish with hands until transparent
      • Use like magnifying glass with sun
      • Difficult but possible
